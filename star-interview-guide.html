⭐ INTERVIEW FRAMEWORK (STAR+L METHOD)

S - Situation: Set the context. When, where, what was happening? Be specific.

T - Task: What was YOUR responsibility? What goal were you working toward?

A - Action: What specific steps did YOU take? Use "I" not "we."

R - Result: What was the outcome? Quantify with data/metrics.

L - Learnings: What did you learn? How did it change your approach going forward?

Key Rules:

  • Use "I" not "we" when describing actions
  • Quantify results with numbers (%, time saved, team size, incidents resolved)
  • Keep answers to 2-3 minutes max
  • Prepare 2 stories per principle - some stories can cover multiple principles
  • End with impact and what you'd do differently if relevant

👤 LP 1: CUSTOMER OBSESSION

"Leaders start with the customer and work backwards."

Common Questions:

  • Who was your most difficult customer?
  • Tell me about a time you didn't meet customer expectations.
  • How do you prioritize customer needs with a large number of customers?
  • Tell me about a time you went above and beyond for a customer.

Story 1: Fixing Azure Free Trial Customer Satisfaction Crisis

S: When I took over the Azure Customer Support Services team (ASMS) with 20 engineers, customer satisfaction (CSAT) scores were critically low. The team was underperforming and customers were frustrated.

T: I needed to identify root causes of low satisfaction and turn the metrics around while maintaining team morale and operations.

A: I conducted a full audit of 6 months of ticket data - approximately 3,000 tickets. I categorized them by issue type and low-rated responses. To my surprise, 45% of tickets fell into a single bucket: "Trying Azure with free trial benefit." Customers were getting only automated rejections with no helpful guidance. I:

  • Identified that the root cause was a process gap, not a people problem
  • Redesigned the response templates to provide clear guidance on trial eligibility and alternatives
  • Created knowledge articles explaining usage directions for free trials
  • Retrained the team on empathetic customer communication
  • Implemented a feedback loop to catch similar systemic issues early

R: Customer satisfaction scores improved by 35% within 3 months. The volume of repeat contacts for the same issue dropped by 60%. The team's confidence improved because they now had proper tools and processes to help customers instead of just saying "no."

L: I learned that before blaming people for poor performance, always audit the process. Most customer dissatisfaction stems from systemic gaps, not individual failures. Data-driven root cause analysis applied to customer experience yields massive improvements.

Story 2: VIP Customer Escalation - Overcharge Resolution

S: A customer raised a support request stating they were overcharged (nearly double) for Azure services. The previous engineer failed to provide a proper response, and the customer was already dissatisfied and escalating.

T: As the operations manager, I needed to resolve both the technical billing issue AND restore the customer's trust in our support organization.

A: I personally took ownership of the escalation. I:

  • Reviewed the customer's subscription usage data and billing history in detail
  • Identified the discrepancy in charges and confirmed the overcharge
  • Engaged the billing team immediately to process a refund
  • Called the customer directly to explain what happened, apologize, and outline the resolution timeline
  • Set up proactive alerts on the customer's account to prevent recurrence
  • Followed up within 48 hours to confirm the refund was processed

R: The customer received their full refund, sent an appreciation email to my manager, and remained an Azure customer. They specifically mentioned that the personal attention and speed of resolution restored their confidence. This became a template I used for handling similar VIP escalations.

L: I learned that for high-severity customer issues, personal ownership and proactive communication matter more than the technical fix. Customers remember how you made them feel during the resolution process.

Story 3: Xbox Executive Dashboard Mobile Compatibility

S: In my current role, I created a Power BI dashboard that serves as the single source of truth for our SRE team's performance KPIs (SLA/SLO/SLI analytics). This dashboard was used by executives and C-Suite teams at Microsoft for status reviews. I received feedback that some executives couldn't properly view it on mobile devices.

T: I needed to ensure all stakeholders - including executives who review metrics on-the-go - could access and use the dashboard effectively.

A: I:

  • Immediately prioritized the mobile compatibility fix despite it not being in my sprint
  • Redesigned the dashboard layout for responsive viewing across devices
  • Tested across multiple mobile platforms (iOS, Android tablets, phones)
  • Created a simplified mobile view that highlighted the most critical KPIs executives needed
  • Proactively shared the update with all stakeholders and gathered feedback

R: The executives were able to review operational metrics anytime, anywhere. This improved response time to critical decisions during off-hours incidents. My direct client specifically thanked me for anticipating their needs and making the data accessible.

L: I learned to always consider the end-user experience across all access points. Building something technically correct isn't enough - you need to understand HOW your customer will use it.

🏆 LP 2: OWNERSHIP

"Leaders are owners. They think long-term and don't sacrifice long-term value for short-term results."

Common Questions:

  • Tell me about a time you took on a task beyond your job responsibilities.
  • Tell me about a time you worked on a task with unclear responsibilities.
  • Tell me about an initiative you took on a challenging project.
  • Describe a time you took ownership of a problem nobody else would.

Story 1: Taking Ownership of a Failing Team and Transforming It

S: My senior management gave me a chance to lead an existing team supporting Skype for Business infrastructure and customer migrations from Skype to Teams. This team of experienced engineers was doing everything manually - maintenance, migrations of thousands of customers, and operations documentation.

T: I needed to take ownership of this team's operational model and transform it, despite resistance from senior engineers who felt they were qualified to manage the team themselves.

A: I:

  • Conducted a thorough assessment of all manual processes and their impact on delivery timelines
  • Identified automation opportunities that could eliminate 60%+ of manual work
  • Built a business case with data showing time savings and error reduction
  • Personally designed and implemented PowerShell automation scripts for routine operations
  • Held 1:1s with resistant engineers to understand their concerns and involve them in the solution
  • Created measurable KPIs to demonstrate improvement transparently
  • Took full accountability for the transition, including when early automation scripts had bugs

R: Within 4 months, manual operations dropped by 65%. Migration throughput increased by 40%. The resistant engineers became champions of automation once they saw the data. The team's confidence in my leadership grew because I didn't just delegate - I built solutions alongside them.

L: Ownership means being willing to face resistance head-on with data and empathy. It also means taking personal accountability when things don't work initially, not blaming the team or circumstances.

Story 2: Owning the SRE AI Agent Platform from Concept to Production

S: In my current role at Xbox CXT SRE, I identified that our team was spending 70%+ of their time on repetitive incident remediation tasks that followed known patterns. There was no initiative to automate this - it wasn't in anyone's job description.

T: I decided to take ownership of building an AI-driven automation platform, even though it was outside my core escalation management role and required skills in AI/ML I needed to develop.

A: I:

  • Researched Azure AI capabilities and Logic Apps integration patterns on my own time
  • Built a proof-of-concept SRE Agent that could detect, classify, and auto-remediate common incidents
  • Presented the concept to leadership with projected ROI (time savings, MTTR reduction)
  • Iterated through 3 versions based on feedback, integrating with our existing ICM incident management system
  • Trained the team on using and improving the platform
  • Volunteered to demo the platform across Microsoft PSE teams for broader adoption

R: The Azure SRE Agent now achieves 30-35% automated incident resolution. This freed engineers to focus on complex problems and root cause analysis. MTTR improved by 32%. The platform became a model for other teams at Microsoft. I was recognized for driving innovation beyond my role.

L: Ownership isn't just about current responsibilities - it's about seeing what the organization needs and stepping up to build it, even when it requires learning entirely new skills.

💡 LP 3: INVENT AND SIMPLIFY

"Leaders expect and require innovation and invention from their teams, and always find ways to simplify."

Common Questions:

  • Describe a time you found a simple solution to a complex problem.
  • Tell me about a time you invented something.
  • Tell me about a process you simplified.
  • Describe a time you tried to simplify something but failed.

Story 1: Simplifying 500+ Monitoring Alerts into Actionable Observability

S: When I joined the Xbox CXT SRE team, there were 500+ active monitoring alerts, many redundant, overlapping, or producing false positives. Engineers suffered from alert fatigue - they couldn't distinguish real incidents from noise. There were 25,000+ stale incidents cluttering the system.

T: I needed to simplify our entire monitoring approach so that every alert that fired was actionable and meaningful.

A: I:

  • Audited all 500+ alerts, categorizing each as: critical/actionable, informational, redundant, or obsolete
  • Created a scoring matrix based on: customer impact, frequency, actionability, and correlation with actual outages
  • Eliminated or suppressed 500+ redundant/stale alerts in a phased approach (not all at once, to maintain safety)
  • Consolidated correlated alerts into single incident triggers using Azure Logic Apps
  • Implemented alert suppression during known maintenance windows
  • Created a simple decision tree: if an alert fires, engineers know exactly what action to take within 30 seconds
  • Remediated 25,000+ false-positive/stale incidents from the queue

R: Alert-to-action time dropped from an average of 15 minutes to under 2 minutes. False positive rate decreased by 85%. Engineer satisfaction improved dramatically - they could trust their monitoring. True incidents were identified 3x faster.

L: Simplification doesn't mean removing things - it means making the remaining things more powerful and clear. The best monitoring system is one where every alert tells you exactly what to do.

Story 2: Inventing the Handoff Call Process for Global Operations

S: In my previous Azure LiveSite Operations role, I managed an offshore team of 20 engineers working 24/7 across shifts. Engineers sent handoff emails at shift end, but tasks weren't progressing as expected. Context was being lost between shifts, and issues that should take hours were taking days.

T: I needed to invent a process that ensured seamless knowledge transfer between shifts without adding bureaucratic overhead.

A: I:

  • Analyzed 2 weeks of handoff emails and identified where context was lost
  • Invented a structured 15-minute "handoff call" at each shift transition
  • Created a simple template: What was done, What needs to happen next, What's blocked
  • Made myself available on every handoff call for the first 2 weeks to model the behavior
  • Provided real-time direction when engineers were stuck, eliminating wait time for guidance
  • Iterated the format based on engineer feedback to keep it efficient

R: Task completion time improved by 40%. Client was delighted with the faster offshore delivery. The process became standard practice adopted by other teams. Zero miscommunication incidents after implementation.

L: Sometimes the best inventions aren't technology - they're simple process changes that remove friction from human communication. The key is observing where breakdowns happen and creating lightweight solutions.

🎯 LP 4: ARE RIGHT, A LOT

"Leaders are right a lot. They have strong judgment and good instincts."

Common Questions:

  • Tell me about a time you used judgment to solve a problem.
  • Tell me about a time you worked with incomplete information.
  • Tell me about a time you were wrong. How did you handle it?

Story 1: Quick Judgment During Azure Data Factory Outage - Missing a Second Incident

S: I was the on-call incident manager on a lean Friday. An outage in Azure Data Factory caused a swarm of incidents across our 47 major triggers responsible for Xbox gaming dashboards. During this chaos, I overlooked a high-priority incident from a deployment bug impacting all of West Europe - customers couldn't purchase new subscriptions or process refunds. I only noticed this separate outage 5 hours after initial failure.

T: I needed to acknowledge my mistake, understand why my judgment failed, and prevent recurrence.

A: I:

  • Immediately escalated the overlooked incident once discovered, took personal accountability in the post-mortem
  • Analyzed why it happened: the flood of related ADF incidents masked the unrelated deployment failure
  • Proposed and implemented an "independent incident review" checkpoint during major outages - a 30-minute timer that forces review of ALL new incidents, not just those correlated to the known issue
  • Created alert differentiation rules that flag incidents from unrelated systems even during major events
  • Shared the learning transparently with the team in our weekly review

R: The post-mortem revealed 3,500+ failed subscription purchases and 300+ failed refunds during those 5 hours. After implementing my checkpoint process, we never missed a parallel incident again. The team adopted the "always look for the second problem" mindset.

L: Being right a lot also means recognizing and learning from when you're wrong. Cognitive bias during crisis (anchoring on the known problem) is real and dangerous. Building systematic checkpoints removes the dependency on individual judgment during high-stress situations.

Story 2: Making the Right Call with Incomplete Data on Production Deployment

S: During a routine production deployment for Xbox support services, our monitoring showed an anomaly in a specific region, but the data was inconclusive - it could have been a normal traffic pattern or early signs of customer impact. The deployment team wanted to proceed; they had a deadline.

T: I had to make a call: pause the deployment (risking the deadline) or proceed (risking customer impact) with incomplete data.

A: I:

  • Applied the principle: "When in doubt, protect the customer"
  • Paused the deployment and initiated a 30-minute investigation window
  • Queried KQL telemetry for customer-facing error rates in the affected region
  • Within 15 minutes, confirmed a 2% increase in 500 errors correlating with the deployment
  • Made the call to rollback before impact widened
  • Communicated the decision and rationale clearly to the deployment team and stakeholders

R: The rollback prevented what would have been a Sev-2 incident affecting approximately 50,000 users. The deployment bug was fixed and redeployed successfully the next day. My manager cited this as an example of "good instincts backed by data."

L: Good judgment means having strong instincts AND the discipline to verify them quickly. It also means having the courage to make an unpopular call (pause the deployment) when customer impact is at stake. Speed of verification matters - I didn't wait an hour; I verified in 15 minutes.

📚 LP 5: LEARN AND BE CURIOUS

"Leaders never stop learning and always seek to improve themselves."

Common Questions:

  • Tell me about an important lesson you learned recently.
  • Tell me about a time curiosity led to a better decision.
  • How do you stay current with technology?

Story 1: Learning KQL, Power BI, and AI to Transform SRE Operations

S: When I joined the Xbox CXT SRE project, the team had no centralized reporting or data-driven decision-making capability. All operational insights were anecdotal. I had no prior experience with KQL (Kusto Query Language) or Power BI at this level of complexity.

T: I needed to learn entirely new technical skills to build the observability and reporting infrastructure our team needed.

A: I:

  • Committed to learning KQL from scratch - spent evenings studying documentation, writing practice queries against our telemetry data
  • Enrolled in Microsoft internal training for Power BI advanced analytics
  • Studied Azure AppInsights and Logic Apps for proactive monitoring
  • Built progressive dashboards: started simple (ticket counts), then advanced (trend analysis, predictive patterns, SLA forecasting)
  • Later, when AI became accessible, I taught myself Azure AI capabilities and built the SRE Agent platform
  • Shared my learnings with the team through brown bag sessions, enabling others to build on what I created

R: Within 6 months, I built a comprehensive Power BI reporting suite that became the single source of truth for executive reviews. The KQL skills enabled me to identify incident patterns that were invisible before (leading to the 25,000 stale incident cleanup). The AI curiosity led to the SRE Agent (30-35% automation). I went from zero knowledge to being the team's SME in these areas.

L: Curiosity compounds. Learning KQL led to insights, insights led to the AI agent idea, the AI agent led to automation. Each skill builds on the last. The key is starting, even when you feel unqualified.

Story 2: Learning from a Self-Caused Production Outage

S: During a deployment to production, I made a configuration change that caused an outage. This was early in my career managing production systems and it was the first time I had directly caused customer impact.

T: I needed to resolve the immediate issue, take accountability, and extract maximum learning from the failure.

A: I:

  • Immediately owned the outage and led the resolution, rolling back the change
  • During the post-mortem, instead of being defensive, I dived deep into every contributing factor
  • Identified that there was no validation step between deploying to test/int and production
  • Proposed automated validation alerts for post-deployment health checks
  • Wrote a detailed "lessons learned" document and shared it broadly
  • Volunteered to build the automated post-deployment monitoring system I had proposed

R: The automated health checks I built caught 12 similar issues in the following year before they became outages. The team adopted a "trust but verify" culture for all deployments. I earned more trust from leadership by owning the failure transparently than I would have by being perfect.

L: The biggest learning moments come from failures, not successes. Curiosity about WHY something failed (not just WHAT failed) leads to systemic improvements that prevent entire categories of future problems.

👥 LP 6: HIRE AND DEVELOP THE BEST

"Leaders recognize exceptional talent and willingly move them throughout the organization."

Common Questions:

  • Tell me about a time you mentored someone.
  • Tell me about a bad hire. When did you figure it out?
  • What qualities do you look for when hiring?
  • How do you develop underperformers?

Story 1: Hiring 30 Engineers and Building a World-Class Team from Scratch

S: For the Xbox CXT SRE project, I was responsible for building the entire team from zero. Microsoft gave us a 1-year contract in 2021 to prove our value. I needed to hire 18 engineers in Redmond and 12 in India.

T: I had to hire the right talent, onboard them quickly, define roles and processes, and deliver results fast enough to earn a contract extension.

A: My hiring framework:

  • Defined clear competencies for each role: technical depth, communication, bias for action, curiosity
  • Conducted at least one interview loop personally for every hire
  • Looked for diversity of experience - not just technical skills but problem-solving approach
  • For each new hire, created a 30-60-90 day development plan
  • Post-hire, established weekly 1:1s focused on growth (not just status updates)
  • Identified each person's strengths and matched them to appropriate challenges
  • Created a mentorship pairing system where senior engineers coached juniors

R: The team went from 0 to fully operational within 3 months. We not only met the 1-year contract deliverables but exceeded them - the contract was extended and expanded. I achieved less than 5% attrition over 3 years in a market where 20-25% is normal. Multiple team members received promotions under my leadership.

L: Hiring well is the highest-leverage activity a manager can do. I learned that technical skills can be taught, but curiosity, ownership mindset, and communication ability are innate. I hire for the latter and train the former.

Story 2: Developing a High Performer Who Was Declining

S: An engineer from my previous team asked to follow me to the new Xbox project. He was a top performer - excellent at deep investigation. I hired him. But within 3-6 months, his performance degraded significantly.

T: I needed to understand why a proven performer was declining and help him course-correct before it became a performance issue.

A: I:

  • Didn't jump to conclusions or immediately put him on a PIP
  • Had an honest 1:1 conversation: "I've noticed a change. Help me understand what's happening."
  • Discovered the new role wasn't aligned with his strengths - he was an investigator, but the new role required more proactive building
  • Reassigned him to the incident deep-dive and root cause analysis function where his skills shone
  • Paired him with team members who complemented his weaknesses
  • Set clear, achievable goals aligned with his strengths
  • Checked in weekly to ensure the adjustment was working

R: Within 2 months, his performance returned to top-performer levels. He became our go-to person for complex incident investigations and trained 5 other engineers in his methodology. He specifically thanked me for not giving up on him and for finding the right fit.

L: Declining performance is usually a fit problem, not a talent problem. The best managers don't just develop people - they put them in positions where their natural strengths create maximum impact. My framework: identify competencies, identify deficiencies, map to aspirations, check alignment.

⭐ LP 7: INSIST ON THE HIGHEST STANDARDS

"Leaders have relentlessly high standards."

Common Questions:

  • Tell me about a time you were dissatisfied with quality and improved it.
  • Tell me about a time you motivated others to go above and beyond.
  • Describe a time you couldn't meet your own standards.

Story 1: Transforming Operations Documentation from Chaos to Excellence

S: In my current project, all operations documents, troubleshooting guides, and onboarding materials were stored in OneNote. They were unreliable - we couldn't quantify usage, there was too much obsolete data without differentiation, and new team members were confused regularly. This caused delays in incident resolution because engineers couldn't find accurate procedures.

T: I set a standard: every troubleshooting guide must be current, validated, and findable within 30 seconds during an incident.

A: I:

  • Audited all existing documentation - categorized as current, needs-update, or obsolete
  • Archived (not deleted) all obsolete content to maintain history
  • Defined a documentation standard: each guide must have an owner, last-validated date, applicability scope, and step-by-step procedures
  • Migrated from OneNote to a structured platform with search, version control, and usage analytics
  • Authored 175+ troubleshooting guides personally in 5 months to set the quality bar
  • Implemented quarterly documentation reviews where engineers validate their owned guides
  • Created templates so new guides followed the standard automatically

R: Documentation findability went from "ask someone" to "self-service in under 30 seconds." New engineer onboarding time reduced by 40%. Repeat tickets for known issues dropped by 55% because engineers (and the AI agent) could find resolution steps immediately. Usage analytics showed guides were accessed 2,000+ times per month.

L: Highest standards means building systems that make the standard easy to maintain, not just achieving it once. Documentation debt is technical debt - it compounds silently until there's a crisis.

Story 2: Raising the Bar on Incident Response SLAs

S: When I took over, our team's SLA compliance was at 85% - meaning 15% of incidents were not being addressed within the agreed timeframes. Leadership had accepted this as "good enough" for years.

T: I refused to accept 85% as acceptable. My standard: 98%+ SLA compliance with improving MTTR quarter over quarter.

A: I:

  • Analyzed the 15% of missed SLAs to understand patterns: time of day, complexity, staffing gaps
  • Discovered that most misses happened during shift transitions and weekends (coverage gaps)
  • Redesigned on-call schedules to eliminate coverage gaps
  • Created escalation automation: if an incident approached SLA breach, auto-escalate 15 minutes before deadline
  • Implemented daily backlog reviews - no incident goes unowned for more than 4 hours
  • Made SLA performance visible: real-time dashboard on the team's shared screen
  • Celebrated improvements publicly while coaching privately on misses

R: SLA compliance went from 85% to 98% within 6 months. MTTR improved by 32%. The team took pride in their performance metrics. The project was recognized as a model for operational excellence within the broader Microsoft PSE organization.

L: High standards aren't about being harsh - they're about making success achievable. When you remove the barriers to performance (coverage gaps, unclear escalation paths, invisible metrics), people naturally perform at a higher level.

🚀 LP 8: THINK BIG

"Leaders create and communicate a bold direction that inspires results."

Common Questions:

  • Tell me about your most significant professional achievement.
  • Tell me about a time you had to make a bold decision.
  • Tell me about a time your vision led to great impact.

Story 1: Envisioning and Building the AI-Driven SRE Platform

S: In 2023, our SRE team was handling thousands of incidents monthly, many following predictable patterns. The industry was talking about AIOps but nobody in our org had implemented it. I saw an opportunity to fundamentally change how incident management works - not just incrementally improve, but transform.

T: I envisioned a future where 50%+ of routine incidents would be auto-detected, auto-classified, and auto-remediated without human intervention - freeing engineers for strategic work.

A: I:

  • Created a vision document outlining the AI SRE Agent concept and 3-year roadmap
  • Phase 1: Auto-detection using pattern recognition on telemetry data
  • Phase 2: Auto-classification and routing based on historical patterns
  • Phase 3: Auto-remediation for known resolution patterns
  • Presented to senior leadership with projected ROI: 35% engineer time savings, 50% MTTR reduction
  • Built Phase 1 proof-of-concept personally to demonstrate feasibility
  • Got buy-in to proceed and iterated through multiple versions
  • Demoed across Microsoft PSE teams, creating a movement beyond my own team

R: The platform currently achieves 30-35% automated resolution (Phase 1-2 complete). It's being evaluated for adoption by 5+ other Microsoft teams. Engineers now focus on complex problems and innovation rather than repetitive remediation. Leadership cited this as the most significant operational innovation in our org.

L: Thinking big isn't about having a perfect plan - it's about having a bold vision and the courage to start building toward it. The key is demonstrating early wins (proof-of-concept) while communicating the larger vision. People rally behind a vision when they can see it working.

Story 2: Azure Live Chat Initiative - From Zero to Scaled Support Channel

S: Microsoft wanted to introduce Live Chat support for Azure Subscription Management - a completely new support channel with minimal tooling and no data on how it would perform. This was a big bet on a new customer experience model.

T: I was asked to envision and execute the launch of this new channel for a team that had never done chat-based support.

A: I:

  • Designed the entire operating model: staffing, routing, escalation, quality monitoring
  • Created chat-specific training (chat support requires different skills than phone/email)
  • Built performance metrics from scratch: response time, resolution rate, CSAT, concurrent sessions
  • Started small with 3 agents, proved the model, then scaled to the full team
  • Partnered with engineering to provide feedback on tooling gaps
  • Established quality bar: every chat transcript reviewed for first 2 weeks, then sampling

R: The chat channel launched successfully, became a preferred support channel for customers, reduced average handle time by 45% compared to phone, and improved CSAT by 12%. The model was adopted by other Azure support teams.

L: Thinking big with a "start small" approach reduces risk. I learned that new channels need their own metrics, training, and processes - you can't just bolt them onto existing ones. The biggest success came from treating it as a product launch, not just a process change.

⚡ LP 9: BIAS FOR ACTION

"Speed matters in business. Many decisions are reversible and don't need extensive study."

Common Questions:

  • Tell me about a calculated risk you took.
  • Describe when you took initiative to correct a problem without being asked.
  • Tell me about a time someone wasn't responsive and you had to act.

Story 1: Taking Over a Leaderless Escalation Team During Crisis

S: The manager of one of our escalation teams left the organization suddenly. This team managed a critical internal chat bot used by Microsoft Azure customers for platform deployments. Within days, I received a call that the chat bot had failed - customers were experiencing degraded performance.

T: Nobody asked me to own this. The team was leaderless and the system was failing. I needed to act immediately.

A: I:

  • Took ownership of the situation within minutes, not days
  • Assembled the team on a bridge call and assumed the incident commander role
  • Triaged the issue: identified it was a backend service connection failure
  • Directed specific engineers to specific troubleshooting paths (not "let's all look at it together")

  • Implement larger changes with team buy-in
  • Establish regular feedback mechanisms (retrospectives, skip-levels)
  • Set clear, achievable goals and celebrate progress
  • Invest in professional development (training, stretch assignments)

Q: How do you prioritize when everything is urgent?

A:

My prioritization framework:

  • Customer impact (how many users affected? Revenue at risk?)
  • Time sensitivity (SLA breach imminent? Degrading situation?)
  • Effort required (quick win vs. multi-day investigation?)
  • Dependencies (is anything else blocked on this?)

I use a 2x2 matrix: Impact vs. Urgency

  • High impact + High urgency: Do NOW (Sev-1 incidents)
  • High impact + Low urgency: Schedule this week (process improvements)
  • Low impact + High urgency: Delegate or automate (routine requests)
  • Low impact + Low urgency: Backlog (nice-to-haves)

The key discipline: resist the tyranny of urgency. Not everything marked "urgent" is high impact.

🤖 HOW I'VE ADOPTED AI IN MY WORKFLOWS

Opening Frame (10 seconds):

"I've adopted AI across three layers of my work: proactive detection, automated resolution, and operational intelligence. Let me walk through each with specific examples."

Example 1: Agentic AI for Incident Auto-Remediation (The Big One)

S: In my current role managing SRE operations for Xbox support services, we were handling thousands of incidents monthly. 70%+ followed predictable patterns — the same root causes, same resolution steps, repeated by engineers manually every time.

T: I saw an opportunity to build an AI-driven automation platform that could detect, classify, and auto-remediate incidents without human intervention.

A: I:

  • Designed and built the Azure SRE AI Agent using Azure Logic Apps, AppInsights, and AI pattern recognition
  • Trained the system on historical incident data — the 175+ runbooks I'd written became the AI's "playbook"
  • Built a 3-phase approach: Phase 1 (auto-detect anomalies), Phase 2 (auto-classify and route), Phase 3 (auto-remediate known patterns)
  • Integrated it with our ICM incident management system so it operates within existing workflows
  • Built human-in-the-loop guardrails: the AI acts autonomously on high-confidence matches, escalates to humans on low confidence

R: 30–35% of incidents now auto-resolve without human touch. Engineers focus on novel, complex problems. MTTR improved by 32%. I demoed this across Microsoft PSE teams for broader adoption.

L: Building AI for operations requires starting with high-quality knowledge (runbooks) as training data, implementing confidence thresholds for autonomous action, and always maintaining human escalation paths. The AI augments humans — it doesn't replace judgment.

Example 2: AI-Powered Proactive Monitoring (Predicting Before Users Notice)

S: Our traditional monitoring was reactive — alerts fired AFTER something broke. We were always chasing problems.

T: I wanted to shift from reactive to predictive — identifying degradation patterns before they became customer-impacting incidents.

A: I:

  • Used Azure AppInsights with anomaly detection to identify unusual telemetry patterns (latency spikes, error rate drift)
  • Built KQL queries that analyze trend deviations against baselines — not just "is it broken?" but "is it trending toward broken?"
  • Created Logic App workflows that auto-trigger investigation playbooks when predictive thresholds are hit
  • Set up correlation rules: if Service A shows early degradation AND Service B has a deployment in progress → likely related, auto-pause deployment

R: We now catch ~40% of issues before users report them. Mean-Time-to-Detect dropped from "after customer complaint" to proactive alerting within minutes of anomaly onset.

L: Predictive monitoring requires understanding your baseline deeply. The AI isn't magic — it's pattern deviation detection done at machine speed. The key investment is defining what "normal" looks like for each service.

Example 3: AI in Daily Operational Intelligence (Power BI + KQL)

S: Executive reviews required me to synthesize operational data from multiple sources into actionable insights — ticket trends, SLA performance, recurring issues, staffing gaps.

T: I needed to move from manual report creation (hours per week) to automated, AI-augmented intelligence.

A: I:

  • Built Power BI dashboards with AI-powered smart narratives that auto-generate written summaries of trends
  • Used KQL with machine learning functions (series_decompose_anomalies, autocluster) to automatically surface:
  • Unusual ticket volume spikes by category
  • Emerging incident patterns not yet in our runbooks
  • Team workload imbalances
  • Created automated weekly insights emails that highlight "what changed this week" without manual analysis
  • Used AI clustering to identify that 25,000+ incidents were stale/false-positive — leading to the massive cleanup

R: Report generation went from 4 hours/week to automated. Executive reviews are now data-driven with AI-surfaced insights I would have missed manually. The stale incident discovery alone saved hundreds of engineering hours.

L: AI for intelligence is about surfacing what humans would miss at scale. The 25,000 stale incident discovery is a perfect example — no human would have manually reviewed that volume, but AI clustering made it trivial.

Example 4: Generative AI for Knowledge Management at Scale

S: When authoring 175+ runbooks in 5 months, I needed to produce high-quality documentation at a pace that wouldn't normally be possible.

T: Accelerate knowledge capture without sacrificing quality or accuracy.

A: I:

  • Used generative AI to draft initial runbook templates from incident resolution notes
  • AI-generated first drafts of troubleshooting decision trees based on historical resolution patterns
  • I then validated, refined, and enriched each document with expert knowledge
  • Created consistent formatting and structure across all 175+ documents (AI ensured uniformity)

R: 175 guides in 5 months — roughly 3x the pace I could have achieved manually. Quality remained high because AI handled structure/formatting while I focused on technical accuracy. These documents then became training data for the SRE AI Agent, creating a virtuous cycle.

L: GenAI is a force multiplier for knowledge work when you maintain human validation. The virtuous cycle (documentation → AI training → automation → more documentation needs) is the most powerful outcome.

Closing Statement (10 seconds):

"So AI isn't just a tool I've adopted — it's something I've built, deployed to production, and proven at scale. I think about AI adoption in three tiers: AI that detects (observability), AI that acts (automation), and AI that informs (intelligence). The best results come from combining all three."

  • TPM roles → Focus on program structure of AI delivery (roadmap, phases, cross-team coordination)
  • SRE roles → Focus on technical implementation (Logic Apps, KQL ML functions, confidence thresholds)
  • Operations roles → Focus on outcomes (30-35% automation, MTTR reduction, proactive detection)
  • DevOps roles → Focus on CI/CD integration and deployment correlation
  • ITSM roles → Focus on how AI improves incident/problem/change management processes
